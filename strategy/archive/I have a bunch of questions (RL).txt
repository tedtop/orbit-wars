The reframe: behavior is visible even when code isn't

  You said "if we can't see what they're doing, how can we BC them?" — but we can see what they're doing. We just can't see how. Every prize-zone team's (obs, action) at
  every step is sitting in Kaggle's daily episode dumps, which your own pull_topbot_episodes.py already harvests. BC, IRL, and offline RL all consume behavior, not source
  code. And two things never actually happened yet:

  - BC has never been run on the prize-zone data. bc_train.py's own caveat says it's only been validated on local low-rating play. So "BC didn't work" is premature — it
  hasn't been tried on good data.
  - The "reverse-engineering that failed" wasn't a policy learner. replay_analysis.py is a behavioral profiler — reaction times, phase volumes, comet-targeting rate. It
  was never trying to recover a policy; it produces descriptive stats. Useful, but for a different job (opponent-conditioning, below).

  You're right that naive BC caps at the teacher. Here's how to exceed it from their logs.

  1. Recover their objective, not their actions (my top pick). Their move in each state is a revealed preference — it beat every alternative they passed up. We already own
  the candidate generator (build_target_shortlist + the scoring pipeline), so for any replay state we can regenerate the same waves they'd weigh, and fit a scoring
  function whose top-ranked wave matches their actual move (a simple pairwise "chosen > not-chosen" ranking loss — tractable, data-efficient, not fragile IRL). Then we
  plug those recovered weights into orbit_lite's scorer and let our deeper planner (clairvoyant/helmsman) pursue their goal better than they do. Same objective, better
  optimizer = you beat the teacher. This is literally "what are the top teams optimizing for" — the thing reverse-engineering wanted but couldn't read off code.
  2. The teacher we own. The "BC can't beat the teacher" ceiling only bites if the teacher is external and fixed. The entire public top family is orbit_lite presets — we
  have that code. So tune the config (CMA-ES/Optuna) in the gym until it beats the public presets, and that tuned engine is a teacher ≥ the public bots. BC it, then
  self-play past it (the AlphaGo recipe: imitation is the floor, self-play is the climb). Our teacher is internal and rising.
  3. Mistake-mining — beat them where they're weak. orbit_lite has the exact combat-flow projector. Run the top bots' replay states through it and flag the moves that were
  dominated (declined a +EV capture, walked into the do-nothing blind spot). The delta is a labeled "their mistake → the correction" dataset. Train on it and you're
  strictly better than them in exactly their failure modes.
  4. Offline RL (IQL/CQL) is the textbook tool for "exceed the logging policy from fixed logs" — but it's data-hungry and finicky, so I'd stage it after 1–3 prove the data
  is good.

  The honest meta-point: the private leaders are almost certainly also forward-planning engines with better tuning and a couple of bolt-ons — not some secret algorithm. We
  can't copy their config, but we can (a) infer their objective from behavior, (b) out-tune them in our own gym, and (c) exploit the structural blind spots they almost
  certainly still share. None of that needs their code.

  The four bots

  clairvoyant (predicts the enemy's next move → folds it into the projection), kingmaker (4P: hit the leader, let rivals bleed), helmsman (searches its own future turns
  instead of greedy), maestro (the v2-gru controller slot, finally with a real controller). Each is a one-change fork of comet_reaper, A/B'd against it.
